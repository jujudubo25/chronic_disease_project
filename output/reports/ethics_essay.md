The first data processing/analytical decision we made in this project was to remove missing data (i.e., remove rows where BMI, Glucose, or BloodPressure = 0). We decided, upon cleaning, that there was no missing data in our diabetes individual dataset. However, if we decided to remove rows with presumed (but incorrect) missing data, that could be problematic for observations that had meaningful data. This data would tell the story of whether a certain individual, in a certain race/ethnic group, had diabetes, hypertension, or a certain BMI- all factors that go into risk assessment in various locations/groups. Essentially, minority groups could be harmed if their data were wrongfully removed. If we had identifiable patient data, we would ensure that the researcher entered the data correctly and that our codes accurately reflected the removal of observations that were actually missing.
A second decision we made was to categorize the individuals into thresholds: for example, into BMI classifications (underweight, normal, overweight, and obese). From there, we cross-tabulated by clinic to see that there were more obese people in clinic than in clinic 2, and more underweight people in clinic 2 than in clinic 1, for example. Using BMI categories to identify higher risk for a given outcome (diabetes, in our context) can affect who gets access to certain screening/preventative care. This might be harmful for those who are at the cutoff between thresholds but not considered "at risk" enough to be flagged for high risk. With identifiable patient data, we'd want to consider all of their raw data, including continuous BMI results, before making decisions that could affect their access to care.
A third decision we made was to identify the top 5 counties by diabetes prevalence. We decided, upon our output, that Orleans County had the highest diabetes prevalence (0.14), while St. Tammany County had the lowest diabetes prevalence (0.09). However, the total population is included when measuring prevalence. If some of the counties' diabetes cases are amongst very few people, the prevalence might look higher compared to another county that has a large number of people. Also, only the prevalence of diabetes is a factor when identifying these top counties, when the other contextual factors of the county should be considered. If certain counties have a prevalence that appears to be low, people in those communities could be harmed if they are excluded from preventative/screening care. If we had identifiable patient data, we would look at the county priority with multifaceted decision-making, taking more than just prevalence (and total population of the county) into account.
The perpetuation of health disparities applies to the NHANES dataset and populations in Orleans Parish because large datasets like NHANES, that represents the U.S. population, might not be representative of those living in Orleans Parish. Essentially, the communities in the U.S. differ by state, city, county, and town. In our diabetes dataset, we counted 46 Non-Hispanic Black people, 52 Non-Hispanic White, 51 Non-Hispanic Asian, 43 Hispanic, and 52 who are other/multi-racial. This demonstrates diversity amongst race/ethnic groups, so applying models based on biased/non-representative death can yield less-than-accurate predictions for certain minority groups. Their risk might be underestimated, leading to a lack of screening/intervention.
Our ethics commitment will include:
1.    	The practice of utilizing models that are unbiased and representative of the population of interest
2.    	Working with de-identified data to ensure confidentiality and privacy
3.    	Ensuring that assessments are unbiased and reported with transparency and care to the general public.
