Q1. PRIVACY AND RE-IDENTIFICATION
Even without names, combining age, gender, BMI, and race could potentially re-identify someone in a small Orleans Parish neighborhood, as these key demographics could be enough for someone to assume an identity if neighbors are close with one another. It's important to take key demographic data into account when dealing with real data, even if names are not given. According to the HIPAA Safe Harbor Provision, the process of de-identification includes de-identifying specific identifiers of that individual (i.e., the combination of factors such as age, gender, BMI, and race), so that the covered entity cannot identify the individual. This data would be considered protected health information (PHI) if it can be used to identify the individual. A specific safeguard to implement before sharing this data with an external collaborator would be to group age into age bands (> 30, 31-44, etc.), BMI into classified groups (underweight, normal...), and race into a broader race/ethnicity group. That way, the data in those key demographics aren’t specific and identifiable, where it could be traced back to the individual.

Q2. INFORMED CONSENT AND SECONDARY DATA USE
(a) If a health department used the high_risk_adults list for targeted outreach, informed consent could be a major issue. Initially, individuals might have been informed that their data would be collected for reported metrics or collective measures to improve access for certain counties or communities. However, the person might not have consented to further inquiry and communication if their health data flagged them into a certain sub-group (high risk). Even if a person is at high risk, they might not have consented to home visits or phone calls, even if the intentions of the researcher are to help. It is important for the researcher to not go beyond what the data was initially collected for and to collect informed consent for what the data will be used for.
(b) The original survey consent does not extend to algorithmic flagging and intervention. Purpose limitation is a core data protection principle, ensuring that those in control of the data are only using it for specific purposes, not using it for any excessive or unauthorized purposes. Essentially, If the individuals consented to the original survey, it does not mean they consented to algorithmic flagging and intervention.
 Source: https://responsum.eu/glossary/purpose-limitation/

For the most part, our race-group analysis showed similar levels of diabetes prevalence across racial categories. Additionally, since this seems to be clinic-based data, it may not take into account the true population distribution as those being seen at a clinic may be more likely to have health conditions and other comorbidities compared to the general population. The model could potentially misestimate the risk for different subgroups, especially if some may have hypertension or diabetes risk at lower BMIs. Also, the model does not differentiate between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes, and T1D is a genetic, autoimmune disorder which would not have any of the same risk factors as T2D, so if the model is not differentiating between the two, it would either over or under predict the risk for different individuals.

Q5. DATA STEWARDSHIP
In our script, we cleaned rows where BMI, glucose, or BP were equal to zero. Thankfully, 0 is not a plausible number for any of these biological markers, meaning that we would not be deleting any “true” data. The zero value, from a biological standpoint, should not be different across any subgroups. However, if some groups are likely to have this information missing, such as denying being weighed at the doctor, not wanting a glucometer to measure their blood glucose levels, or denying a blood pressure reading, they would not be included. This, however, could be resolved by inputting these values as null or missing, and not by leaving them as 0. 0 is biologically implausible for all these values, so in order to have the most accurate model possible, we would need to delete them.
