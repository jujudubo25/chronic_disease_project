Surveillance Brief for the Orleans Parish Health Department
This investigation has consisted of analyzing vital diabetes and hypertension data amongst 1,000 individuals in four counties of Louisiana: Orleans, St. Tammany, Jefferson, and East Baton Rouge. This analysis aimed to examine diabetes and hypertension prevalence, associated risk factors, key demographics, and high-burden counties. Our goal is to determine which high risk-populations would benefit most from a targeted intervention to improve health outcomes.
Before analysis, we prepared the data files to ensure no missing values and created tables to determine how many patients were in each Clinic, and how many of each race/ethnicity had the outcome.
Additionally, we created different age groups (<30, 30-44, 45-59, or 60+) and BMI groups (classified as underweight, normal weight, overweight, or obese) to assess how risk would differ amongst factors like age and BMI.
We found that there is a high prevalence of diabetes and hypertension in each Clinic, with a diabetes prevalence of  50.8% in Clinic 1 and 49.8% in Clinic 2. Similarly, there was a hypertension prevalence of 51.6% in Clinic 1 and 51.4% in Clinic 2.
When looking at each race/ethnicity group, the other/multi-racial group had the highest diabetes prevalence of 54.4%, followed by Non-Hispanic Whites at 50.9%, Non-Hispanic Blacks at 50.2%, Non-Hispanic Asians at 49.7%, and Hispanics at 47%.  The other/multi-race group also had the highest prevalence of being a triple burden (being obese, hypertensive, and diabetic). Those under 30 also had the highest prevalence of hypertension at 52.5%.
An observed health disparity from the race/ethnicity-specific results was an indicated disproportionate burden on those of other/multi-race, as they had the highest diabetes and triple-burden prevalence, compared to the other race/ethnicity groups.
A targeted intervention for our high-risk group (those being 45-64 years, obese, physically inactive, and hypertensive) could be hour-long community exercise sessions, three days a week, with each individual being matched with a lifestyle coach to promote exercise, nutrition, and overall wellness.
A limitation of this investigation is the lack of generalizability, as only two Clinics were included in our assessment. Health characteristics and key demographics might differ amongst various clinics throughout Louisiana.
For future direction, we will analyze and collect data from more Louisiana clinics, both geographically and racially dispersed, to ensure representativeness.
To map our work to the Harvard 5-phase workflow: Ask > Get > Explore > Model > Communicate, we followed this process to ensure clarity, reproducibility, and efficiency in our data:
 
Ask: What is the prevalence of diabetes and hypertension amongst different Louisiana counties, and how does it differ by race/ethnicity, Clinic, and age?
 
Get: We collected the data through computer packages, utilizing datasets with individual diabetes, county, and race/ethnicity data.
Explore: Upon exploration, we found a disproportionate burden amongst those of other/multi-race, and those who are below 30. Also, we identified individuals who are considered high risk, and those who are considered a triple burden.
Communicate: The development of this surveillance brief to present our key findings and discuss actionable interventions as a result of our data.
